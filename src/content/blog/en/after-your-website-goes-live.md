Most business owners treat launch day as the finish line. In practice it's closer to the start of the site's working life — it's the point where real visitors, real content updates, and the odd unexpected issue actually begin.

## What You'll Be Able to Do Yourself

Before I hand anything over, I walk you through how to make the basic changes yourself — updating opening hours, swapping a photo, adding a new listing or product, publishing a quick announcement.

Most clients aren't technical, and that's completely fine; the goal is that the small, everyday changes don't need to wait on me.

## The Post-Launch Support Window

Every project also includes a fixed window of post-launch support — [X weeks] from go-live — for anything that pops up that isn't a case of you learning the ropes: a broken link, a form not sending properly, something rendering oddly on a particular phone.

That window exists because real usage always surfaces a handful of small things a design and build phase can't fully predict.

## What's Covered — and What Isn't

Bug fixes and small adjustments to what was already agreed and built — yes, that's included.

Brand new features, extra pages, or a different design you've decided you want after seeing the live site in action — that's a new, separately scoped piece of work, quoted the same fixed-price way as the original project.

## The Handover Itself

The handover itself is usually a short session, not a manual you're expected to read cover to cover. I'll show you the specific things you're likely to actually touch — your services, your hours, your photos, maybe a blog if you're using one — and skip the parts of the admin area you'll probably never need.

The goal is confidence for the handful of edits you'll genuinely make, not a full technical education.

## If You'd Rather Not Touch It At All

For clients who'd rather not touch the site at all after handover, there's a monthly retainer option instead — a set fee, no lock-in contract, covering ongoing changes and small updates as they come up.

It tends to get faster over time too, simply because I already know the site and the business by then, rather than starting cold on every request.

## Don't Forget the Boring Stuff

There's also the boring-but-important stuff nobody thinks about until it breaks: domain renewal dates, hosting renewals, and basic uptime.

I flag those before they lapse for clients on a retainer, and for everyone else I make sure you know exactly what's due and when before I step back, since everything stays registered in your name regardless of how we work together afterwards.

## A Real Example

A construction-supply business updates its seasonal catalogue a few times a year. Rather than learning a content-management system for something that infrequent, they use a small block of retainer time each season — a few emails back and forth, updated within a day or two, and otherwise the site just runs quietly in the background.

## Keeping an Eye on the Numbers

It's also worth checking in on the numbers occasionally, even informally — how many people are visiting, which pages they actually spend time on, whether the contact form or booking flow is being used the way you expected.

You don't need to become a data analyst; a quick look every few months is usually enough to catch a page that's quietly not working the way everyone assumed it was.

Going live isn't the end of the conversation, it's the point where the site starts actually working for your business — and having a plan for what happens next, even a simple one, is what keeps it useful instead of quietly going stale.
