The most common assumption I hear on a first call is "our website looks old, we need a whole new one." Sometimes that's true.

More often, the site is fine underneath and what's actually broken is much smaller and much cheaper to fix. Here's how to tell the difference before you spend on either.

## When a Tune-Up Is the Right Call

A tune-up is usually the right call when the bones are sound: the site works on mobile, the structure makes sense, but it loads slowly, the content is stale, the photos are outdated, or nobody at the business can quite figure out how to update it themselves.

All of that is fixable without touching the underlying build — a performance pass, a content refresh, a bit of retraining on the admin panel.

## When a Full Rebuild Is the Right Call

A full rebuild is the right call when the platform itself is the problem: it's on software so old it's a security risk, it genuinely doesn't work on phones (not "looks a bit small," but broken), it can't represent what the business does now versus what it did five years ago, or nobody can safely make changes without something breaking elsewhere.

Patching that kind of site is usually more expensive over time than rebuilding it properly once.

## Two Real Examples

A construction-supply business came to me convinced they needed a full new site. Once I looked under the hood, the actual problem was a bloated, disorganised product catalogue and a slow host — a restructure and a speed fix solved it for a fraction of a rebuild's cost, and they kept the site they'd already invested in.

A real estate business, on the other hand, was running on a platform that had been discontinued by its original developer years earlier — no security updates, no mobile support, and nobody left who could safely touch the code. There, a tune-up would have just been patching a sinking boat. A rebuild was the honest answer, even though it wasn't the cheaper one.

## How I Actually Decide

I look at the hosting and underlying platform first, then run the site through basic performance and mobile checks, then read through the actual content against what the business does today.

That takes me maybe half an hour, and it's enough to tell which category a site falls into before we talk about any spend at all.

## A Quick Self-Check You Can Run Yourself

Open your site on your own phone and try to actually use it — book something, find your phone number, read your services. Then check when it was last updated versus how long the business has been operating differently since.

If the gap between what the site says and what the business actually does now is small, you're probably looking at a tune-up. If the site describes a business that doesn't really exist anymore, that's a rebuild conversation.

## Be Honest About Which One You Have

It's worth being honest with yourself about which one you actually have, because the cost difference is significant — a tune-up is typically a fraction of a rebuild, and it's usually faster too.

If your gut says "new website," it's worth getting a second opinion first. Sometimes the cheap option really is the right one, and I'll tell you that even when it means a smaller invoice for me.

## The One Trap Worth Naming

A tune-up on a platform that's genuinely past its use-by date is money spent delaying the inevitable, not saving it.

If the underlying software can't be updated safely, no amount of content polishing fixes that — that's the one scenario where I'll push back on a tune-up even if it's what a client originally asked for.
