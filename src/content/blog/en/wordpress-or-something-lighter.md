"Should we be on WordPress?" comes up in almost every first conversation, usually because someone's told the client it's either the only sensible choice or something to avoid entirely.

The honest answer is: it depends on how your business actually uses its website day to day, not on which platform is trendier.

## When WordPress Earns Its Place

WordPress earns its place when a business needs to change content itself, often. If you've got staff updating listings, posting news, adding blog content, or managing a calendar of events every week, WordPress's admin panel and plugin ecosystem make that genuinely easy without needing a developer involved every time.

## What WordPress Costs You in Return

What WordPress costs you in return is ongoing maintenance — plugins need updating, security patches matter more because it's a bigger, more common target, and it's easy for a site to end up slower than it should be once enough plugins are stacked on top of each other.

None of that is a dealbreaker, it's just a real, ongoing cost that needs to be part of the decision, not a surprise afterwards.

## The Lighter Alternative

The lighter alternative — a modern static-style build, the same approach this site is built on — trades some of that day-to-day editability for speed, lower hosting costs, and a much smaller security footprint. There's less that can go wrong because there's less moving under the hood.

The trade-off is that content changes typically go through me (or whoever built it) rather than a simple content box the client edits directly, unless we specifically build in a lightweight way for them to do it.

## The Questions I Actually Ask

In practice, I ask a few direct questions before recommending either way: who's actually going to update content, and how often? Does the business need a blog or regular listings? What's the appetite for an ongoing maintenance conversation versus wanting to set it and mostly forget it?

## Two Real Examples

A healthcare clinic that updates opening hours, staff bios, and the odd announcement most weeks was a clear fit for WordPress — the admin panel earns its keep there.

A construction-supply business and a real estate brochure site, where the content changes only a handful of times a year, were both better served by a lighter static build — faster for visitors, cheaper to run, and there was simply less need for a full content-management system sitting underneath a site that barely changes.

## Speed Is Worth Being Specific About

A well-built static site will generally load noticeably faster than an equivalent WordPress site running the same design, purely because there's less happening on the server every time a page is requested.

For most small businesses that difference isn't going to make or break a sale, but for anyone relying on search ranking or running ads to the site, faster load times are one of the few genuinely free wins available.

## It's Not Always a Permanent Decision

I've moved clients from WordPress to a lighter build once their content settled down and they realised they weren't actually using the editing flexibility they'd paid for, and I've done the reverse for a business that outgrew a static site once they started publishing content weekly.

The platform should follow how the business actually behaves, and that can change.

Neither platform is the "correct" one in the abstract. The mistake is picking based on fashion or fear rather than matching the tool to how the business actually operates. That's a conversation worth having before any design work starts, not after.
