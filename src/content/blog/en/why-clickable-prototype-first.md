        "A client once described their new site to me as \"pretty simple, just show our listings and contact details.\" Once they clicked through an actual working prototype of that \"simple\" site, it became obvious the listings page needed to work completely differently to what either of us had pictured from a written brief alone. That's a normal, healthy part of the process — and it's exactly why I don't write a single line of code until that conversation has happened.",
        "Every project starts in Figma. You see full designs of every page, and you can click through a working prototype of the site before any development begins — clicking between pages, opening menus, scrolling through a listings page, the works. It behaves like the real site, even though nothing is actually built yet.",
        "The reason this matters is cost, plain and simple. Changing a Figma frame takes minutes — move a section, swap a layout, rethink how a page flows. Changing the same thing once it's built in code takes hours, sometimes days, because now there's logic, responsive behaviour, and other pages depending on it. Catching a problem at the design stage is cheap. Catching the same problem after launch is expensive and stressful for everyone.",
        "An anonymised example: a real estate client reviewing their prototype realised halfway through clicking their own listings page that agents needed to see photos before price and suburb, not after — the opposite of what the initial brief had described. Because it was still a prototype, that was a ten-minute change. If we'd already built it, it would have meant reworking a live template across every listing.",
        "This also changes the working relationship, not just the budget. Feedback happens early, when it's still cheap to act on, instead of arriving as a list of \"can we change this\" requests after the site is live and everyone's moved on mentally. By the time development actually starts, there are no real surprises left for either of us — we've both already seen and agreed on what's being built.",
        "It also means the build phase moves faster than people expect, because I'm not guessing at unresolved design questions while writing code — every screen has already been agreed on, clicked through, and signed off. The result is a shorter overall timeline even though there's an extra step at the front of it.",
        "This works just as well for clients I never meet in person. Most of my feedback rounds happen over a shared link and a written comment or a short call, not a meeting room — you can click through the prototype on your own time, mark up what's working and what isn't, and send it back. It suits business owners who are trying to run a business at the same time as briefing a website, which is most of them.",
        "It doesn't mean unlimited revisions at the design stage either — that would just move the cost problem somewhere else. What it means is that the revisions that do happen are cheap ones, made before anything is built, instead of expensive ones made after.",
        "If a designer skips straight to code without showing you something clickable first, you're the one absorbing the cost of every change that surfaces later. Seeing it before it's built isn't a nice extra — it's what keeps the whole project cheaper and less stressful, for you and for me.",
        "Got a question about your own site? Ask me — no obligation."
