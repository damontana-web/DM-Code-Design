        "The clients whose projects go fastest and cost the least aren't the ones with the biggest budgets — they're the ones who show up to the first call with these five things already sorted. None of it takes long, and it makes the brief conversation genuinely useful instead of a guessing game.",
        "First, know who the site actually needs to convince. \"Everyone\" isn't an answer — a real estate site trying to reassure a first-home buyer reads very differently to one aimed at investors, and a construction-supply site talking to trade accounts needs different information up front than one talking to weekend renovators. Knowing your actual reader shapes everything from the homepage layout to what goes above the fold.",
        "Second, gather your content before the brief, not after. Text and photos are the single biggest driver of how long a project takes — I can design and build around finished content in a fraction of the time it takes to wait on drafts, rewrites, and \"I'll send that photo later.\" If writing copy isn't your thing, at least have rough notes and existing photography ready; it's still faster than starting from nothing.",
        "Third, know who owns your domain and hosting, and have the logins handy. A surprising number of small businesses don't actually know who registered their domain or where it's hosted — sometimes it was set up years ago by an employee who's since left, or by whoever built the last site, and nobody's touched it since. Sort this out early and it's a five-minute step instead of a delay that stalls the whole project waiting on account recovery.",
        "Fourth, bring two or three reference sites you genuinely like — and be specific about why. \"I like the layout of this one\" or \"I like how simple this one's navigation is\" is useful. \"It just looks nice\" isn't, because it doesn't tell me what to actually build toward. It's just as useful to bring a site you actively dislike and say why — knowing what to avoid narrows things down just as fast as knowing what to aim for. Specifics turn a vague brief into a clear one.",
        "Fifth, know your real budget range before the call. Not because I'll spend all of it regardless — I'll always quote a fixed price for what you actually need — but because knowing the range up front means I can shape a proposal that fits your business, rather than presenting the maximum version and negotiating down from there.",
        "One thing that sits underneath all five: figure out who else needs to sign off before work starts, and get them involved from the first call rather than partway through. If the website decision actually needs a business partner, a manager, or a family member's agreement, a brief that only reflects one person's view tends to unravel once the second decision-maker sees the designs for the first time. That's the single most common cause of a project stalling midway that has nothing to do with the design itself.",
        "You don't need to nail all five before you reach out — some of this genuinely gets sorted on the first call together. But the more of it you've thought through beforehand, the less that call is spent on logistics and the more it's spent actually talking about your business.",
        "None of this is complicated, and you don't need to have it perfectly polished. But turning up with even a rough version of these five sorted means our first call is about your business and what the site needs to do, not about chasing down basics — and that usually means a faster project and a smaller invoice.",
